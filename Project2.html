THE SOLAR SYSTEM

SUN

The Sun is a massive, glowing sphere of hot plasma located at the center of our solar system. Composed primarily of hydrogen and helium, it serves as the primary source of energy for all life on Earth. Through nuclear fusion at its core, the Sun converts hydrogen into helium, releasing an enormous amount of energy in the form of light and heat. This energy radiates outward, affecting the entire solar system and driving climate, weather, and the cycles of life on our planet. As a G-type main-sequence star (G2V), the Sun has been shining for approximately 4.6 billion years.

Beyond its vital role in sustaining life, the Sun exerts a powerful gravitational force that keeps the planets, asteroids, comets, and other celestial bodies in orbit. Its immense mass—accounting for more than 99.8% of the total mass of the solar system—creates a stable gravitational center. The Sun’s activity, including solar flares and coronal mass ejections, also influences space weather, which can impact satellites, communication systems, and power grids on Earth.

MERCURY

Mercury is the smallest and innermost planet in our solar system, orbiting the Sun at an average distance of about 58 million kilometers (36 million miles). Despite being the closest planet to the Sun, it is not the hottest—Venus holds that title due to its thick atmosphere. Mercury has a rocky, barren surface covered with craters, ridges, and plains, resembling Earth's Moon. VENUS

Venus, the second planet from the Sun, is often called Earth's "sister planet" due to its similar size, mass, and composition. However, its environment is drastically different and far more hostile. Venus is shrouded in a dense atmosphere composed mainly of carbon dioxide, with clouds of sulfuric acid, creating an intense greenhouse effect that traps heat. This makes Venus the hottest planet in the solar system, with surface temperatures soaring above 460°C (860°F)—hot enough to melt lead.

03

Venus rotates very slowly and in the opposite direction to most planets, including Earth, meaning the Sun rises in the west and sets in the east. One day on Venus (one full rotation) lasts longer than its year—about 243 Earth days compared to its 225-day orbit around the Sun. Despite these extremes, Venus has captivated scientists for decades. Its surface features include vast volcanic plains, towering mountains, and evidence of past tectonic activity.

EARTH

Earth is the third planet from the Sun and the only known celestial body to support life. It has a unique combination of a breathable atmosphere, abundant liquid water, and a stable climate, all of which make it exceptionally well-suited for sustaining a diverse range of organisms. Earth's atmosphere is composed primarily of nitrogen and oxygen, providing essential gases for life and shielding the planet from harmful solar radiation through the ozone layer. About 71% of Earth’s surface is covered by water, which plays a critical role in regulating temperature, supporting ecosystems, and shaping weather patterns. In addition to its physical characteristics, Earth has dynamic geological activity, including plate tectonics, which continuously reshapes its surface and contributes to the carbon cycle that stabilizes climate over long periods. Earth's single natural satellite, the Moon, helps moderate the planet's axial tilt, contributing to relatively stable seasons. Earth completes one orbit around the Sun in about 365.25 days and rotates on its axis every 24 hours, creating the cycle of day and night.

MARS

Mars, the fourth planet from the Sun, is often called the "Red Planet" due to its reddish appearance, caused by iron oxide—commonly known as rust—on its surface. Slightly more than half the size of Earth, Mars has a thin atmosphere composed mostly of carbon dioxide, which provides little insulation or protection from solar radiation. As a result, surface temperatures are cold, often ranging from -125°C (-195°F) at the poles during winter to about 20°C (68°F) at the equator during the day. Mars has seasons, polar ice caps, and features such as valleys, deserts, and the largest volcano in the solar system, Olympus Mons, as well as the vast canyon

Evidence gathered by orbiters, landers, and rovers—like NASA’s Curiosity and Perseverance—suggests that Mars once had liquid water on its surface and possibly conditions suitable for microbial life. The search for signs of past life and understanding Mars’ climate history are central goals of ongoing missions. With its relatively accessible terrain and proximity to Earth, Mars remains a focal point of scientific discovery and a symbol of humanity’s ambition beyond.

SATURN

Saturn is the sixth planet from the Sun and the second-largest in our solar system, right behind Jupiter. It's a gas giant made mostly of hydrogen and helium, and it's known for its stunning system of rings, which are made of ice, rock, and dust particles. Though other planets like Jupiter and Uranus also have rings, Saturn’s are by far the most prominent and complex, stretching out over 170,000 miles but only about 30 feet thick. This mighty planet spins fast, completing a full rotation in just over 10 hours, which makes it bulge slightly at the equator. It has over 140 confirmed moons, with Titan

With its strange storms, wild magnetic field, and mysterious interior, Saturn remains one of the most fascinating worlds out yonder in space. Saturn continues to captivate stargazers.

JUPITER

Jupiter is the largest planet in our solar system, more than twice as massive as all the other planets combined. It's a gas giant composed primarily of hydrogen and helium, with no solid surface. One of its most striking features is the Great Red Spot, a massive storm system larger than Earth that has been raging for centuries. Jupiter’s rapid rotation completing a full spin in just under 10 hours causes it to bulge at the equator and fuels powerful jet streams across atmospheres. Beyond its impressive size and storms, Jupiter

07

is also home to a vast system of moons—over 90 confirmed—with four major ones called the Galilean moons: Io, Europa, Ganymede, and Callisto. Each of these moons is unique: Io is the most volcanically active body in the solar system, while Europa may harbor a subsurface ocean that could support life. Ganymede is the largest moon in the solar system, even bigger than Mercury. Jupiter’s intense magnetic field, sprawling ring system, and dynamic atmosphere make it a key focus of planetary research.

URANUS

Uranus is the seventh planet from the Sun and one of the coldest in the solar system, with temperatures dipping below -350°F (-210°C). It's an ice giant, made mostly of water, methane, and ammonia ices, wrapped in a thick atmosphere of hydrogen and helium. The presence of methane gives Uranus its pale blue-green color. One of the planet’s most unusual features is its tilt — Uranus is tipped over on its side, rotating almost perpendicular to its orbit. This extreme tilt causes it to experience the most dramatic seasons in the solar

system, with each pole getting 42 years of continuous sunlight followed by 42 years of darkness. Uranus also has a faint ring system and at least 27 known moons, most of which are named after characters from Shakespeare and Alexander Pope. Though it's not as well-studied as Jupiter or Saturn, Uranus remains a fascinating target for future exploration. Its off-kilter rotation, icy composition, and mysterious interior structure pose many questions that scientists are eager to answer.

NEPTUNE

Neptune is the eighth and farthest known planet from the Sun in our solar system. It’s an ice giant like Uranus, made mostly of water, ammonia, and methane ices, with a deep atmosphere of hydrogen and helium. Its striking deep blue color comes from methane in the upper atmosphere, though the exact reason for its richer hue compared to Uranus is still being studied.
